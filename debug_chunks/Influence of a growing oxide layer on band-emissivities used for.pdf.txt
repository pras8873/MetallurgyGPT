Influence of a growing oxide layer on band-emissivities used for optical temperature measurements Wolfgang Bauer*, Alexander Moldenhauer*, Friedbert Rogge** *Universität Duisburg-Essen, 47048 Duisburg, Germany, w.bauer@uni-duisburg-essen.de ** TRW Automotive GmbH, Barsinghausen, Germany ABSTRACT It is known from theory that growing oxide layers on metal surfaces effect interferences of the spectral emissivities. These interferences can strongly change with increasing thickness of the oxide layer, because their maximum and minimum values shift to longer wavelengths and their intensities become larger or smaller. The changes of spectral emissivities also lead to changes of the band-emissivities used as values for emissivity correction of pyrometers or IR- cameras. Primarily, these effects depend only on emissivity and can occur without changes of metal’s temperature. For the working ranges of the optical temperature instruments follows, that the values of band-emissivities may change constantly with growing oxide layers. This will result in problems for an accurate temperature measurement. At the University of Duisburg-Essen it is now possible to carry out in-situ measurements of the spectral emissivities of growing oxide layers on metals. These investigations can be done under technically relevant conditions of heating time, and temperature. Low alloyed steel with a growing oxide layer was investigated. The steel sample

--- CHUNK 1 ---
was heated up to 1150°C within a period of 2 minutes and, further, tempered for 1 minute more at this temperature. During this period, the oxide layer was steadily growing and the changes of spectral emissivities were measured at several increments of time. It was found, that the theoretically predicted interference effects, their changes and shifts in the spectral properties are readily seen and could be measured. Results will be presented of the spectral emissivities from 0.7 to 25 µm and of based on it calculated band-emissivities between 0.7 to 4.5 µm, which are in correlation with the working ranges of three chosen pyrometers. Strong and relatively fast changes of the spectral emissivities can be achieved with values between 0.6 and 0.9. Therefore, during the growth of the oxide layer accurate temperature measurements with optical instruments make no sense because of the permanent change in the values of band-emissivities. An exact determination of the temperature under these circumstances is not possible. Only weak influence on the values of band-emissivities were found when correlated with the spectral response functions of the chosen pyrometers. MEASUREMENT DEVICE FOR SPECTRAL EMISSIVITIES The measurement device (fig.2) is designed for measuring temperature-dependent normal spectral emissivities. The principle of the measurements consists in the direct comparison of the spectral intensities of the radiation emitted by a sample with those of a black body source at the same temperature and under identical optic-geometrical conditions [8, 9]. Fig. 2: Scheme of the device for spectral emissivity measurements As black body radiators several tube furnaces for different temperature ranges are in use. Two of these furnaces are fitted with heat-pipes. These furnaces have sufficient high emissivities about 0.99 and better. They are electrically heated and their temperatures are PID-controlled. For temperature measurements inside the tubes thermocouples and PT 100 sensors are in use. The metallic samples are warmed up by direct electrical resistant heating in a water cooled bell furnace. The surface temperatures of the samples are measured with thermocouples and fixed by PID-control. Inside the bell furnace vacuum or measuring atmospheres of air, inert, or

--- CHUNK 5 ---
protective gases can be generated. The wall temperature of the bell is stabilized and also PID-controlled. The bell has a flange with an optical window which is necessary for escape of the Proc. of SPIE Vol. 7299 72990P-2 sample radiation. Further the laboratory is air conditioned to keep constant room temperature. The FT-IR spectrometer has been modified by the manufacturer for measuring of external radiation sources. Therefore, it is additionally equipped with an optic box for identical focusing of the external radiation sources to realize the emissivity measurements under identical optical and geometrical conditions. To keep the optical bench encapsulated to the laboratory air, this box further has an optical window for infrared radiation. The spectrometer is installed on a vehicle to measure first the spectrum of the black emitter at a certain temperature, and afterwards the spectrum from the sample at the same temperature. From these data the spectral emissivities are computed. The FT-IR-spectrometer is able to measure a spectrum in few seconds, so samples with changing emissivities during heating up can be investigated. Temperature range for the measurements is 100 °C up to 1600 °C, down limited by the sensitivity of the spectrometer and capped by the black

body temperature, or the melting points of the investigated metals. The maximum spectral range is about 0.7 to 25 µm, depending on temperature and emissivity of the sample. Spectral resolutions of 2, 16 and 64 wavenumbers are used depending on measurement conditions. The uncertainty of the emissivity determinations is mainly influenced by the uncertainty of the measurement of the surface temperature of the samples. Furthermore, the uncertainty is depending on the ranges of the measuring temperatures and wavelengths. The relative uncertainty of the measured emissivities of metals for this device is in average about 5.8 %. 3. SPECTRAL EMISSIVITIES OF AN OXIDIZING STEEL SAMPLE A selected low alloyed steel grade with a growing oxide layer was investigated. The sample was heated up to 1150°C within a period of 2 minutes and, further, tempered for 1 minute more on this temperature. During this period, the oxide layer was steadily growing and the changes of spectral emissivities were measured at several increments of time. CONCLUSIONS It was found,

--- CHUNK 13 ---
that the theoretically predicted interference effects for oxidizing metal surfaces with their changes and shifts in the spectral properties are readily seen and could be measured in the spectral emissivities, also under technical conditions of heating time, and temperature. In the wavelength range from 0.7 to 4.5 microns, which means in the work areas of many pyrometers and IR-cameras, strong and relatively fast changes of the spectral emissivities can occur, which lead to permanent changes in the values of band-emissivities. Therefore, during the growth of oxide layers accurate temperature measurements with optical instruments make no sense, exact determinations of the temperature under these circumstances are not possible. Only weak influence on the values of the computed band-emissivities were found when correlated with the spectral response functions of selected pyrometers. Finally it can be concluded that these results are valid for two-color and multi-wavelength pyrometers too. Also in these cases during oxidation processes the values of spectral emissivities may change constantly for all working wavelengths of the devices and lead to problems with temperature measurements.
