July 2008 ✦ 29 S lab centerline quality requirements are becoming increasingly more demanding, especially with greater applications for line- pipe grades, which require high strength and toughness, good weldability (e.g., off- shore applications) and are resistant to hydro- gen-induced cracking (HIC), particularly in sour gas service. Similarly, other grades that require optimum centerline conditions and are finding greater applications include trans- formation induced plasticity (TRIP) and dual- phase steels. It has also been found that slabs with minimum centerline segregation are more microstructurally homogeneous in sub- sequent rolling and other thermomechanical processes. Therefore, there are clear incen- tives to improve centerline condition for a number of applications and processing steps. One of the most effective ways to achieve this improved centerline condition, which is so crucial to producing the above-listed value-added steels, is the application of soft reduction. More aggressive machine taper- ing, the principle behind soft reduction, has been practiced in the steel industry for several decades with some success. The key has always been to control a vast number of casting con- ditions to a narrow band of acceptable limits that would ensure the final point of strand solidification within a defined area of the machine containment

section. The complex- ity of scheduling with the meltshop, and con- trolling variables such as tundish superheat, has made the task extremely difficult to repeat with regularity. The introduction of Dynamic Soft Reduction, whereby the machine contain- ment geometry can be changed as required, is making the application of soft reduction more in-tune with the ever-changing conditions that influence strand casting. The machine geometry is varied via hydraulically gapped segments. Each segment is gapped with four clamping cylinders, which are equipped with position feedback sensors that work in con- junction with the automation control system to ensure that the roller spaces are correct for soft reduction practice. Equally important to the control of the seg- ments is the ability of the automation system to accurately predict the final point of solidi- fication of the strand. A computer model is used to gather operating variables, and con- trol the secondary cooling system to establish the crater endpoint and properly gap the soft reduction segments. The amount of soft reduction, and the length over which soft reduction is applied, is dependent on steel grades and specific chemical composition. Centerline Segregation and Porosity Centerline segregation is typically observed as localized inhomogeneities in the

--- CHUNK 2 ---
last por- tion of steel to solidify: the center area of the strand. Previous studies have detailed that segregation spots are caused by mass flow of solute-enriched liquid solidification contrac- tions during the final stage of solidification.3 Stated another way, the between-roll strand bulging enables molten steel to flow in the casting direction, which induces the accumu- lation of impure molten steel at the crater end.2 Observed typically from sulfur prints or macro-etches, centerline segregation can be enlarged and clustered, a.k.a. “macrosegrega- tion,” or can be seen as “islands” of segrega- tion, a.k.a. “semi-macrosegregation.”4 As the final solidification proceeds, there is also a chance of porosity, which is due to an inad- equate liquid pool available to feed the solidi- fying matrix, leaving voids at the centerline of the cast strand. Soft Reduction Rate and Amount It is well-documented that excessive machine tapering or soft reduction will result in regions of both positive and negative segregation. Negative segregation is caused by areas where strand reduction is excessive, resulting in squeezing of the strand centerline. Moreover, excessive machine tapering can result in damaged rolls and bearings, the extent of which depends on the caster roll- er and bearing arrangement, material type (roll stress tolerance) and roll condition. The machine tapering is typically expressed in terms of mm per meter length of machine, but is also sometimes expressed in terms of mm taper per minute, the latter typically with regard to machine loading. Still, the amount of soft reduction required to optimally provide for measurably improved centerline condition is not only the rate of soft reduction (mm per meter, or mm per minute), but also the total amount of soft reduction applied. There is also an optimum amount of total reduction, as excessive amounts of soft reduction are known to actually result in mass flow of unsolidified, solute-enriched

--- CHUNK 5 ---
steel moving into adjacent bulged areas. According to L.K. Chiang, for a strand thickness of 203 mm (nominal), a threshold value exists that corresponds to a strand reduction ratio of 0.974, which is close to the ratio of steel volume contraction on the liquid-to-solid phase transformation, and excessive amounts of soft reduction can result in “type A” segregation.3 From the literature, it can be inferred that, depending on grade mix, width, thickness, machine design and operating conditions, the optimum soft reduction amounts range between 0.60 and 1.0 mm per meter. Also, one needs to be clear that the amount of soft reduction is tailored to account for the sum of (a) solidification shrinkage and (b) bulging that occur. This does not take into account thermal shrinkage, for which thermal tapering is applied. Under normal operating conditions, the cylinder position is maintained and the seg- ments are in “position control.” The cylinder position is generated from the following considerations: • Thermal taper. • Soft reduction taper. • Operational loading. • Calibration offset. Thermal taper is applied due to the normal shrinkage of the solidifying strand. Soft reduc- tion taper is applied to provide the additional force that is necessary to reduce centerline segregation and porosity. Operational load- ing is the compensation for the bending response of the segment due to the ferrostatic pressure of the solidifying strand during cast- ing conditions. Under extreme or abnormal casting condi- tions, such as a bulged strand or a transition strand, if any cylinder experiences a strand width–dependent maximum or minimum force, it will enter force control. In force control, the cylinder that is being affected will automatically abandon its position setpoint and respectively lift or lower to relieve or apply the appropriate pressure to the strand. This active segment protection, or overload protection, increases the life of the bearings and rollers. Cylinder Position Setpoint First, the cylinders must be calibrated in order

to correlate position transmitter feedback into an actual, physical position. The calibration procedure is performed by applying a small amount of force to a calibration spacer of known thickness that has been placed within a segment gap. The calibration offset that is obtained is applied to the position transmitter feedback. Once the calibration is established, then the segment entry and exit gaps are set based on adjacent segments, steel grade solidification characteristics and casting conditions. Operational Loading During casting, the ferrostatic pressure of the solidifying strand will actually cause the seg- ment to open slightly. To compensate for this phenomenon, an offset is dynamically applied to the cylinder position setpoint, which is width dependent, and characteristic of the mechanical bending properties of the seg- ment and its cylinders. This offset is necessary to overcome the ferrostatic pressure and set the cylinders at a position that is slightly lower in order to achieve the appropriate roll gap between the rolls of each HSA segment. Soft Reduction As most elements solidify, they have a lower solubility in the solid phase than in the liq- uid phase, causing them to be rejected into the liquid. During the solidification process, solidification occurs in the process of tree- like dendrites forming and growing out from the solidified shell. The elements that solidify last most likely end up doing so in the arms of these dendrites, which leads to voids. These voids cause detrimental centerline segregation and porosity in the middle of the steel strand at the final point of solidifi- cation. The elements that contribute most to this quality problem due to their solubility are carbon, manganese, sulfur and phospho- rous. Application of Dynamic Soft Reduction on Existing Machines Today’s steel market demands higher produc- tion levels and constant improvement in strand quality. In many cases, existing machines are casting grades that will benefit from the appli- cation of Dynamic Soft Reduction, but these casters do not currently have this capability. Machines with either an extraction section (Figure 4) or large-diameter roller segments (Figure 5) have a large roller pitch that allows the strand to bulge in the area of final solidifi- cation, leading to centerline segregation. In the case of the extraction section, the machine can be converted to accept new seg- ments capable of Dynamic Soft Reduction. In some cases, the existing roller support frames can be re-used, keeping the duration of the conversion outage to a minimum while achiev- ing maximum improvement. In the case of existing segments, a few options are available for upgrading to soft reduction. In one instance, the segments may be suitable for accepting new hydraulic clamping cylinders outfitted with the proper electronics to enable Dynamic Soft Reduction. The main criteria for this revamp consider-

--- CHUNK 15 ---
ation are the structural strength and integrity of the existing segment frames, and the roller diameter and spacing. If the segment is not suitable for conversion to perform Dynamic Soft Reduction, then replacement segments, equipped for Dynamic Soft Reduction capabilities, can be supplied. In many cases, existing casters have horizontal segments containing five rollers, which can readily be replaced with gap-controlled seg- ments arranged with an increased number of smaller-diameter, more tightly pitched rollers.
