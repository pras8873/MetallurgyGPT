Introduction Continuous casting (CC) [1] ( Fig. 1 ) is the process of solidifying molten steel into a semi-ﬁnished products, usually slabs, billets or blooms, which are used further in rolling or extrusion processes. The molten steel is ﬁrst transferred from the ladle to the tundish, and then through the submerged entry nozzle (SEN) in to the

--- CHUNK 2 ---
copper mold. The outer surface of the mold, which is closed at the bottom, is water cooled with high ﬂow rates. Solidiﬁcation of the molten steel starts immediately and the solid shell, deﬁning the ﬁnal shape of the billet, is formed. When the strand exits the mold, the thickness of the solid shell is large enough to hold the molten steel, subject to ferrostatic and other types of forces. The strand is pulled from the mold with the withdrawal unit into the secondary cooling system, containing the spray chamber and support- ing rolls. At the end of the casting machine, the completely solidiﬁed strand is torch-cut into the desired lengths. Defects in the strand, such as cracks, pin-holes, and shape defects, originate mainly in the mold, where strong natural and forced convection with solidiﬁcation occurs ( Fig. 2 ). Radial basis functions [33] are used in LRBFCM for trial functions in combination with strong formulation. EMS is a common technique used to improve the quality of CC prod- ucts. The eﬀect of EMS on the quality of steel depends on the position of the stirrers and their electromagnetic parameters. The main bene- ﬁt of the strand EMS is larger central equiaxed zone, while the ﬁnal EMS (at the metallurgical length) mitigates the centerline segregation. Mold EMS improves the surface quality, lowers the macrosegregation, promotes columnar to equiaxed transition, enhances solidiﬁcation, and creates more uniform thickness of the solidiﬁed shell, thus enabling the increase of the casting speed and reducing chances for breakouts. Conclusion This paper represents meshless numerical procedure for solution of the 3D turbulent ﬂuid ﬂow with solidiﬁcation under the presence of the EMS. The numerical procedure is divided into two separate parts. The ﬁrst part is the pre-processing stage, and assumes the solution of the EM ﬁeld by the FEM software Elmer [62] , while the main part, elaborated in the present paper, represents the meshless numerical procedure for the solution of the velocity and temperature ﬁelds. Our novel in-house developed meshless numerical method, based

--- CHUNK 43 ---
on the LRBFCM, is in the present work further developed to solve 3D problems with adaptive- shape RBFs and adaptive-displacement upwind. The inﬂuence of the EM ﬁeld on the velocity ﬁeld is considered by the averaged Lorentz force, calculated from the solution of the ﬁrst part, and used in the second model in the momentum equation as an external force. The geometry, material properties and boundary conditions of the CC process are sim- pliﬁed in order to enable straightforward comparison with other nu- merical approaches in the future. The parametric study of the eﬀect of changes in EM ﬁeld on velocity and temperature ﬁelds was performed. The developed simulation system will be used for optimization of the 361 R. Vertnik, K. The problem un- der consideration is most probably the largest published problem solved up to now by LRBFCM, as it is computed on 473080 nodes. The paper also demonstrates the ability of the meshless method to solve realistic industrial turbulent ﬂuid ﬂow problems with complex swirling ﬂow

In the future, the numerical model will be upgraded to solve the species transport equation and thus be able to obtain the macrosegre- gation, which also eﬀects the quality of billet.
