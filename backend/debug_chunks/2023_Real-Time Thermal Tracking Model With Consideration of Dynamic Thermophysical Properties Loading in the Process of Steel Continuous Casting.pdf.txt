ORIGINAL RESEARCH ARTICLE Real-Time Thermal Tracking Model With Consideration of Dynamic Thermophysical Properties Loading in the Process of Steel Continuous Casting XING XIA, CHENG JI, and MIAOYONG ZHU The accurate online determination of the solidiﬁcation of continuously cast strands is the primary condition for the eﬀective implementation of solidiﬁcation end reduction (SER) and electromagnetic stirring (MES). Thermal tracking calculations based on the ﬁnite diﬀerence method (FDM) or ﬁnite volume method (FVM) are the most popular methods for monitoring the strand solidiﬁcation process. In the traditional models, the thermophysical properties of steel are determined from only temperature dependence; the calculations do not consider the eﬀects of uneven solute distribution on the casting strand. In this study, a real-time carbon distribution-dependent thermal tracking model (CDD-TTM) was developed. The model could online calculate the solidus temperature, liquidus temperature, heat conductivity, density, and enthalpy according to the carbon segregation of continuous casting strand both on the longitude and cross-sections. Furthermore, the feedback eﬀect of SER was considered also, which extended the contact length between rollers and slab and reduced the thickness of the strand. The calculation results were veriﬁed by infrared temperature measuring experiment, and the maximum relative error of surface temperature is 2.11

INTRODUCTION CONTINUOUS casting, a highly eﬃcient process for casting molten steel, is used in more than 99 pct of steel global steel production. Center segregation and porosity are two of the most signiﬁcant problems aﬀecting the quality of continuous casting steel. Technologies such as soft reduction (SR), heavy reduction (HR), and electro- magnetic stirring (EMS) have been widely applied to the solidiﬁcation process to improve the quality of strands. Accurate prediction of the solidiﬁcation process is an essential precondition for the eﬀective application of these advanced technologies.[1] Numerical simulations of the heat transfer are the most popular method used to evaluate the solidiﬁcation process of the interior of strands during continuous casting. In 1967, Mizikar et al.[2] was the ﬁrst to solve the heat transfer diﬀerential equation by a numerical method. Many researchers subsequently established two-dimensional and three-dimensional solidiﬁcation heat transfer models[3–12] and solved them using ﬁnite diﬀerence,[4–7] ﬁnite element,[8,9] ﬁnite volume,[10] and meshless methods.[11,12] Due

--- CHUNK 2 ---
to the variation of continuous casting conditions, such as casting speed, temperature, and steel grade, scholars developed a variety of real-time heat transfer models for online systems.[13–19] In the 1990s, Voest-al- pine industrieanlaenbau (VAI)[13] developed Dynacs (Dynamic Continuous Casting Cooling Management System) for unsteady casting processes. It could predict the solidiﬁcation process under unsteady casting condi- tions. Many researchers have derived and applied boundary conditions for heat extraction in molds,[20] water ﬂux distributions in secondary cooling processes,[21,22] and roller contact in air cooling[23,24] according to experimental and simulation results. The thermophysical parameters of metals are mainly deter- mined by solution composition and temperature. Many researchers[25,26] have proposed a microscopic segrega- tion model of solid/liquid and d/c interfaces during steel solidiﬁcation. These models can be used to quantita- tively calculate the solute phase fraction at diﬀerent temperatures. Ueshima et al.[25] used a microscopic segregation model to quantitatively calculate the solute phase fraction during solidiﬁcation. The thermophysical parameters of microstructure phases in steel can be regarded as functions of temperature. They can be accurately calculated by combining the phase frac- tions.[5,27–29] Hahn et al.[27] proposed methods (Dyna- Phase) to calculate the Gibbs free energy of multicomponent phase diagrams and quantitatively analyze the interdendrite solidiﬁcation process. They applied the methods to calculate the thermophysical property parameters of steel. Xie et al.[28] developed an algorithm for calculating the thermophysical properties of low-alloy steels. The thermophysical parameters of steel are mainly aﬀected by two factors, temperature and solute. At present, most heat transfer models

--- CHUNK 4 ---
regard thermo- physical properties as dependent on only temperature and usually assume that the solution composition is constant for a speciﬁc steel grade. In the actual continuous casting process, the solute distribution is inhomogeneous in the strand cross-section and along the strand casting direction due to selective crystallization during the solidiﬁcation process. This signiﬁcantly aﬀects the thermophysical parameters, especially for high-carbon or alloyed steels. The present authors[17] previously presented a real-time heat transfer model that considered the carbon distribution in the strand cross-section. The carbon distribution along the center thickness direction of the slab width cross-section was measured experimentally, and the thermophysical parameters at the corresponding locations were calcu- lated and loaded in a real-time heat transfer calculation. However, the two-dimensional carbon distribution in the whole cross-section and its evolution along the casting direction have not yet been considered. The author developed a multiphase solidiﬁcation model[30,31] to study the carbon segregation during continuous casting. The distribution characteristics of carbon elements in the cross-section and the casting direction can be calculated. In this study, as shown in Figure 1, based on the distribution characteristics of carbon elements, a method for dynamic loading of thermophysical properties was presented and applied in the

In terms of boundary condi- tions, the CDD-TTM considers the uneven distribution of secondary cooling water and the reduction on the strand solidiﬁcation process. The CDD-TTM was applied in industry, and the slab inner quality was improved with more accurate predictions of the solid- iﬁcation process. II. Adaptive-Step Algorithm for the Unsteady Casting Process In traditional heat tracking models, new slices are generated at the meniscus of the mold in each calcula- tion period, and slices exceeding the maximum distance are eliminated. When the production speed is low, the slice spacing decreases, which causes slice accumulation and wastes computing resources. This model used the adaptive time step method and set the minimum threshold Z of slice spacing to solve the behavior of slice stacking. Restrictions were added to the process of generating new slices on the meniscus of the mold and conditionally

increased the head slice and eliminated the tail slice (Figure 3). The calculation process of adaptive-step algorithm (Figure 3): (1) Set the minimum slice spacing threshold Dz0, the default time step is Dt, and set the initial slice spacing s ¼ 0. The casting begins. (2) Record the real-time casting speed vi of the current period and calculate the cumulative moving distance Ds: Ds ¼ s þ vi  Dt: ½12 (3) Compare the size of Ds and Dz0, such as Ds  Dz0, indicating that the interval between slices in the current period is too short and enter step 4. If Ds  Dz0, enter Step 5. (4) No new slices are generated in the current cycle, and all slices are shifted back vi  Dt and s ¼ Ds. Enter Step 2 to switch to the next cycle. (5) New isothermal slices are generated at the meniscus in the current cycle. All slices are shifted backward Ds to reset the slice spacing s ¼ 0 and then enter step 2 to enter the next period. C. INDUSTRIAL APPLICATION Based on the solidiﬁcation and heat transfer of the strand, the model has some temperature-related functions: (1) Thermal tracking: Accurate calculation of real-time temperature ﬁelds; display of nonuniform solidiﬁ- cation characteristics of the strand; accurate pre- diction of solidiﬁcation terminal positions; and accurate calculation of solidiﬁcation characteristic curves. (2) Dynamic secondary cooling: On the basis of ﬁrst-level water meter data and combined with the ﬁnite ele- ment temperature at the outlet of each secondary cooling zone of the heat tracking model, the inverse calculation of water volume

--- CHUNK 22 ---
is carried out to complete the adjustment and optimization of water volume. (3) Dynamic reduction: Preset the strategy of reduction of the solid fraction under various working condi- tions. According to the solid fraction curve of the strand and the preset reduction strategy under un- steady production conditions, the reduction parameter is automatically derived. (4) Thermophysical parameter calculation: Based on the microscopic solidiﬁcation model, the thermo- physical parameters can be calculated according to the input steel composition at diﬀerent tempera- tures. The SDD-TTM model has been used in industrial applications.
