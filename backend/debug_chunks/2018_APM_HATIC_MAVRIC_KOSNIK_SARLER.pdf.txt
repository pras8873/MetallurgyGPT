All rights reserved. 1. Introduction Direct chill (DC) casting [1] is the most commonly used technology in the production of aluminium-alloy semi-ﬁnished products. The process involves molten metal being fed vertically through a bottomless, water-cooled mould where it is suﬃciently solidiﬁed around the outer surface to take the shape of the mould and acquire suﬃcient mechanical strength to contain the molten core at the centre. As the strand

--- CHUNK 2 ---
emerges from the mould, water impinges directly from the mould onto the surface (DC), ﬂows over the cast surface and completes the solidiﬁcation [2] . The initial solidiﬁcation occurs in the primary cooling region, where the heat is extracted through the mould. Yet most of the heat is extracted in the direct chill region below the mould, where the water is sprayed directly onto the surface of the billet. to different casting defects, such as inadequate surface quality, porosity, hot tearing and macrosegregation [3] . In addition to alternating the casting parameters, an electromagnetic ﬁeld (EMF) can be induced in order to decrease the quantity of casting defects. The ﬁrst introduction of electromagnetic casting was proposed by the Russian engineer Z. N. Getselev, who proposed a mouldless casting process. The electromagnetic forces and eddy currents in the melt streaming from the high-frequency coil are used instead of the mould to keep the melt in the right position [4] . The main beneﬁt of this process is an improved surface quality, although a decreased level of macrosegregation and a ﬁner grain structure can also be observed. Yet the technology was too complicated to use for large-scale industrial production, mainly due to the regulation process. In the late 1980s the CREM (Casting, Reﬁning, ElectroMagnetic) process was presented by C. Vives [5] . In this process the melt is poured into a mould that is surrounded by a coil. The liquid metal is then stirred with an inductor supplied with a 50-Hz alternating current. The LFEC (Low-Frequency Electromagnetic Casting) was developed by Zhang et al. [6] on the basis of the CREM

--- CHUNK 4 ---
process, where the frequency was further decreased and the hot top was added. It was observed that a reduction in the frequency led to several improvements in the ﬁnished product [6–10] . With the presence of the electromagnetic ﬁeld during the DC casting process, an additional Lorentz body force arises from the electromagnetic ﬁeld in addition to the gravity. The additional force causes the formation of a convex surface meniscus and a decreased contact line with the mould. The Lorentz force is directed perpendicular to the billet surface and therefore has a larger magnitude in the horizontal direction. The magnitude of the Lorentz force is affected by the coil cur- rent intensity and frequency, the number of coil turns, the coil position and the coil size. It mainly intensiﬁes the horizontal mixing process, which results in more constant temperature and concentration proﬁles in the melt [7] . This reduces the size of the central thermo-solutal eddy. The effect can be observed in the rise of the solidus and liquidus lines [7] . The low- frequency EMF and, consequently, smaller billet sump can improve several different properties of the semi-ﬁnished product. LFEC can gradually decrease the level of macrosegregation. In fact,

it was shown that with an appropriate choice of elec- tromagnetic parameters this macrosegregation can be eliminated completely [6] . Among the other beneﬁts of low-frequency- electromagnetic ﬁeld in DC casting are the reﬁnement of the grain structure [8,9] , the reduction of hot tearing [10] , the increased mechanical properties [9,10] and the increased surface quality [10] . However, the quality of the surface in the electromagnetic casting is highly sensitive to the casting and electromagnetic parameters. In addition, there is a substantial need to computationally model the process and a-priory determine the acceptable process conditions. Both research groups have performed simulations for the casting geometry of a simpliﬁed cylinder shape with a constant proﬁle of the melt velocity at the inﬂow. Yet the inﬂow geometry design presumably has a great effect on the solidiﬁcation process. The main purpose of the model presented in this paper is to simulate the LFEC casting process for realistic industrial in- ﬂow requirements using different EMF parameters. The effect of the electromagnetic ﬁeld on the ﬂow pattern is presumably strongly inﬂuenced by the inﬂow geometry, which is investigated in this paper. The main focus of the present paper is a detailed description of the developed computational model and the simulation of the inﬂuence of the EMF parameters and geometry design on the temperature and velocity ﬁelds. 2. Model formulation The physical model of solidiﬁcation is based on the classic mixture-continuum-model approach developed by Bennon and Incropera [13,14] . The general assumptions of the mixture formulation are: the mixture is saturated and it is in local thermal equilibrium. In our model we additionally assume laminar

ﬂow conditions and a constant density for both the solid and liquid phases ( ρs = ρl = ρ). The effect of density changes on the ﬂuid ﬂow is described by the Boussinesq approximation [15] . The cylindrical coordinate ( r , ϕ, z ) system is used to describe the geometry and the physical ﬁelds. Additionally, an axial symmetry assumption is used, which presumes that all the ϕ-coordinate values are equal to zero. All the quantities are therefore described only in the r −z plane. 2.1. The ﬂuid model Liquid and solid phases are both modelled as a pseudo-ﬂuid using Newtonian ﬂuid assumptions. The phase change be- tween the liquidus and the solidus isotherm is modelled using the terms describing the mushy zone, where a rigid solid structure is assumed to form through the packing of the solid grains. The slurry zone is not treated separately and therefore the transport of the ﬂoating grains is neglected. The forces of the solid phase acting on the melt ﬂowing through the porous matrix are simulated by the Darcy-Forchheimer law [16] . Meshless numerical solution A meshless approach is chosen to perform the numerical solution of the conservation and EMF equations. A large number of meshless numerical schemes have been developed in recent decades [20–22] . The main beneﬁt of the meshless methods is that they do not require the generation of polygon mesh elements. Instead, only a set of discretization nodes is required. It can be generated with different procedures. The diffuse-approximate-meshless (DAM) method is applied in the present pa- per; it uses weighted least squares to determine a locally smooth and differentiable approximation of discrete data. Automatic generation of node arrangement The computational node arrangement is generated automatically, after the desired geometry shape is determined. First, the domain geometry is parametrized by a boundary function. The ﬁrst boundary node is located in the lower left corner. The remaining boundary nodes are positioned according to the boundary function in the consecutive order in the counter- clockwise direction. A set of inner boundary nodes is positioned in the next step. The inner boundary nodes are the direct neighbours of the boundary nodes and are positioned in the opposite direction of the outwards facing normal at the bound- ary nodes. The remaining inner points are positioned according to the density function with a Monte Carlo-like procedure. A minimization process is performed in the last step of computational node arrangement. Nodes are rearranged in this pro- cess in order to optimize the distances to the nearest nodes in a similar manner as in the node repel algorithm presented in [35] . The local neighbourhoods consist of 13 nearest neighbours and are created with a k-d tree algorithm [36] . The density function is non-uniform and varies in space. The density is increased in the coil, near the mould, at the

--- CHUNK 20 ---
inﬂow, close to the curved areas of the hot-top, and in the mushy zone. Consequently, the node arrangement also depends on the solidus and liquidus line positions. The node arrangement is adapted automatically when the solidiﬁcation front’s position is changed. The positions of the solidus and liquidus lines at the centre and at the surface of the billet are checked and stored every 20 s of the simulation time. If the difference in the position of isothermals between two consecutive stored values at any of the locations is larger than 5 mm, a new node arrangement is generated. The use of such adaptive variable-density node arrangement signiﬁcantly reduces the total calculation time. 4. Numerical simulations 4.1. Conclusions In this paper, a comprehensive numerical model for simulating the low-frequency electromagnetic casting process is pre- sented. Firstly, the electromagnetic ﬁeld quantities are calculated, followed by a calculation of the momentum, the pressure correction and the energy equation. The equations are numerically solved with the meshless diffuse approximate method. The results show an intense effect of the low-frequency EMF on the solidiﬁcation process. The maximum velocity, simulated under the inﬂuence of the Lorentz force, can be more than 50 times higher than in classic DC casting. Also, the Lorentz body force can be up to 10 0 0 times larger than the thermal buoyancy body force. These effects can greatly increase the mixing process of the molten metal. Effect of the geometry design on the low frequency electromagnetic casting is evaluated for the ﬁrst time with the model presented in this paper. The simulations are performed on the simpliﬁed and realistic geometry and compared to each other. The comparison shows that the effect of geometry should not be neglected. The simulations on plain cylinder

--- CHUNK 44 ---
geometry predict a larger reduction of the sump depth than the simulations on realistic geometry. How- ever, it is observed that the inﬂuence of EMF parameters on the sump depth is similar in both cases, only the magnitude is different. In the next stage of the research the species transport equation will be implemented for investigating the effect of the EMF on the macrosegregation. The macrosegregation model will use the novel operator-splitting algorithm [40,41] to separate the treatment of the transport and the growth stages.
