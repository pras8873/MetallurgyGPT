INTRODUCTION Mechanical soft reduction is a technique to reduce detrimental centre defects in CC blooms, e.g. centre porosity and carbon segregation. The later is a main problem in production of high carbon steel, e.g. bearing steels. Centre porosity is a major problem with low carbon steel. 2. MSR THEORY Soft reduction consists of the application of a small controlled reduction to the strand in the zone where the liquid cone at the final stage of solidification closes up. The deformation by MSR must be sufficient to counteract porosity and segregation, while at the same time not produce cracks in the bloom. The reduction is applied in a progressive series of steps with small reduction carried out at each

--- CHUNK 1 ---
roll. If the reduction is applied too early, the liquid core at the point of reduction will be too large and the stress generated at the solidification front will exceed the critical value for cracking. If reduction is applied too late, the results are similar to rolling a solid bloom. Therefore, the centre porosity can be reduced by implementing a reduction after complete solidification. This technique can be referred to as mechanical hard reduction (MHR). Centre Porosity One purpose of MSR is to close the porosity which forms at the end of the solidification process. Centre porosity occurs especially when the length of the mushy zone is long at the final stage of solidification. Then, the remaining liquid state will not be fed through the bridged arm spacing of the dendrite. MSR will compress the final liquid cone to reduce centre porosity and improve centre soundness. Centre Segregation Another purpose of MSR is to reduce centre segregation. The MSR technology has widely been implemented in CCM since it reduces centre segregation very effectively. MSR is effective in breaking up the interdendritic bridges forming at the cone of the liquid pool. These broken dendrites float in the mushy zone and act

--- CHUNK 2 ---
as nucleation sites for final solidification. The formation of ‘mini-ingots’, which cause macro-segregation at the centreline of the strand, is prevented. 3. MSR CONCEPT AT TIMKEN The MSR processing of blooms is influenced from the following variables:  The thermal distribution in the cross section  The size of the cast profile and consequently the starting size for the calculation of the proper reduction ratio in the first reduction step The Timken Company C. 4202 / 401 Module 8C Page 4 of 15 Rev 2 17/01/2014  The different solidification behaviour due to the chemical composition of the steel grades and to its influence on the variation of liquidus and solidus temperatures  The determination of the final solidification point and consequently of the optimal “reduction window” related to the optimal solid fraction range to perform MSR  Secondary cooling history. The dynamic secondary cooling regulation must assure the same temperature pattern in the cross section for different casting speeds All these features are necessary for the calculation of the solid fraction curve (fs) at the bloom axis and of the optimal “MS Reduction sequence” (see picture below just for reference). The latter is defined as the sequence of optimal

reduction GAPs of each single MSR pinch roll where the GAP is the reduction in mm requested for the single pinch roll. The sum of each single MSR GAP forms the reduction sequence. The picture shows the gradual reduction (in mm) taking place on the pinch rolls. REFERENCE ROLL The reduction is calculated from a reference position. The reference roll is the roll immediately upstream of the first roll engaged in MSR. If the MSR pattern moves upstream or downstream the reference roll moves also. The position of this roll is filtered to give a stable signal. The actual reference value used is marked on the MSR page on the HMI (see screenshot below). the COOL model) All three modes can either be used in force control mode or position control model. STATIC MSR The static MSR is when a MSR Reduction Sequence is defined by the Level 1 HMI and it remains fixed. The MSR pinch rolls will be acting on the blooms with the same gap (position control mode) or force (force control mode) along all the heat or casting sequence without any variation of reduction. SPEED TIE MODE The MSR Sequence will move up and down the caster depending on the average speed of each strand element that is under a given MSR roll at any particular time. In this way, the MSR Sequence approximately follows the solidification profile of the bloom. This mode should be used if the model is not working (for whatever reason). Continuous calculation of the fs (solid fraction) at the bloom axis trough a solidification model (COOL). 2. The translation of the input variables into the output parameters set to correctly perform the MSR process is done with a Dynamic calculation program integrated with a cooling model. The following scheme summarizes the concept. The Timken Company C. 4202 / 401 Module 8C Page 7 of 15 Rev 2 17/01/2014 3.2 CALIBRATION OF MSR ROLLS POSITIONS In order to obtain absolute values of the strand thickness, the position transducers need to be calibrated. This is done using the known thickness of the dummy bar (463 mm for section 460x610mm, and 278mm for section 280x430mm). During start of cast the position transducer of the MSR cylinder is calibrated on the final 615 mm of the dummy

--- CHUNK 6 ---
bar for every roll. The difference between the measured value and the known thickness of the dummy bar is the offset. This offset is written into buffer of the previously measured 10 offsets. If the new measurement is within +/- 0.5 mm of the average of the last 10 measurements, it is accepted and stored in the buffer. The average of the 10 value buffer is the used offset. The Timken Company C. 4202 / 401 Module 8C Page 8 of 15 Rev 2 17/01/2014 3.3 CORRECTION FOR THERMAL EXPANSION OF ROLLS During casting the rolls heat up and expand. Measurements show that the rolls reach temperatures of > 600°C. This leads to a thermal expansion of ~2 - 3 mm. An empirical formula is used to correct for the expansion of the rolls with time during casting and the contraction of the rolls between casts. It is important to note, that the rolls expand differently depending on their position in the caster. This means that not accounting for this expansion would lead to application of wrong reduction amounts when doing MSR. A further important reason for taking into account the thermal expansion of the rolls is to make the

--- CHUNK 7 ---
calibration of the WSUs using the dummy bar more accurate. The Timken Company C. 4202 / 401 Module 8C Page 9 of 15 Rev 2 17/01/2014 3.4 CORRECTION FOR MECHANICAL ELONGATION OF MSR SEGMENTS When the MSR roll is pushed onto the strand, the frame of the MSR unit is elastically stretched by 1.5 mm per 1000 kN of force. Neglecting this elongation would lead to an underestimation of the reduction arriving on the strand. The value of the elastic deformation can be determined by placing a block of hard steel under the MSR roll and running the automatic evaluation routine (see screenshot below). The Timken Company C. 4202 / 401 Module 8C Page 10 of 15 Rev 2 17/01/2014 3.5 SINGLE ROLL CORRECTION AND THERMAL TAPER The amount of soft reduction that is to be applied to the strand must be reduction that is in addition to the natural shrinkage of the strand due to cooling down from MSR module 5 to 11. This means that the thermal taper of the strand (due to natural shrinkage due to cooling) must be added to the soft reduction patterns. Also it is possible that during normal casting without MSR it is

--- CHUNK 8 ---
seen, that a single roll is not properly calibrated. To give the possibility to do position control in spite of this there is the possibility to correct the position of this single roll manually using the “single roll correction”. Care must be taken in using this function as it is often seen that during casting rolls show a positive off-set due to the strand not being fully straightened or due to the reaction force of the straightening of the strand lifting a roll. This phenomenon can be observed when casting hard steels (bearing, spring, etc.) at slow speeds on certain rolls. On the HMI page “Roll Gap”, all corrections are summarized and a bar graph shown of the actual MSR being applied to the strand. When casting without MSR all the bars should be close to zero. This means that the height of the bars during casting with MSR correspond to the net reduction after all corrections, including the natural shrinkage of the strand. PRELIMINARY MEASUREMENTS 5.1 THICKNESS MEASURING The first roll of the MSRs (MSR PR5) is used as thickness measuring device to perform the in line measuring of the thickness of the bloom. The thickness value acts as input for the online soft reduction model and its calculation of the reduction knowing the starting section size according the calculated solid fraction window and fixed position of rolls. 5.2 TEMPERATURE MEASURING SYSTEM The system uses an optical pyrometer to perform the in line measuring of the surface temperature of the bloom. The temperature value acts as input for the online soft reduction model and its calculation of

SOFT REDUCTION PARAMETERS The online bloom solidification model will calculate based on current machine settings (mold heat removal, superheat, casting speed, flow rates, etc.) the bloom centerline solid fraction in these 7 MSR-positions along the caster. MSR shall take place at positions where the calculated centerline solid fraction is in the range 0.30 – 0.80; this range might be changed in future to suit the local conditions existing at the Timken bloom caster as soon as data will be available. An off-line solidification model was used to estimate the bloom centerline solid fraction for determining which MSR units are in the best location from meniscus for applying soft reduction.
