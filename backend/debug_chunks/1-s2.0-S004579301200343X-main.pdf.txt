All rights reserved. 1. Introduction Electromagnetic stirring process has been used from late 1970s in Europe and Japan. Serious researches about an EMS process started from 1980s after the International Union of Theoretical and Applied Mechanics (IUTAM) symposium held in United King- dom [1]. In Japan, the EMS process was selected as one of the most innovative future steel making technologies by the Iron and Steel Institute of Japan (ISIJ) in 1983. Many researches were focused on application of EMS to the continuous steel casting process and its effects on improvement of structure [2], center-line shrinkage [3], surface property [4], intrusion distribution [5], and segregation [6]. The principle of EMS is relatively simple. As molten steel is solidiﬁed, mushy zone is formed between outer solid wall and in- ner molten steel, where liquid and solid coexist. The mushy zone behaves like liquid up to 20% to 30% of solid content if the solidiﬁ- cation structure is not columnar [7]. However, as the solid content increases, dendrites start to grow on

--- CHUNK 2 ---
the solid surface, which has bad effect on the solid structure. Continuous stirring of molten steel can make mushy zone keep liquid characteristics up to higher solid content and the liquid ﬂow of mushy zone can break den- drites and induces grain multiplication [7]. In EMS, the Lorentz force is used to stir molten steel before solidiﬁcation of mushy zone and therefore we can improve steel quality. Even though it looks simple to use EMS in steel casting, there are many unresolved technical problems. The blind use of EMS cannot resolve continuous casting problems and also causes another prob- lem [8]. One typical side effect of using EMS is white bands in billet cross section. This white band is known to come from inhomoge- neous distribution of speciﬁc elements due to insufﬁcient mixing but it is possible to get rid of it with proper control [8]. Another problem in applying EMS is related to the restricted and insufﬁcient stirring. It is reported that insufﬁcient stirring of molten steel can- not stop the growth of dendrites. If the stirring is stopped, the vis- cosity of mushy zone increases dramatically leading to the fast growth of dendrite and solidiﬁcation [8]. Therefore, it

--- CHUNK 3 ---
is important to stir molten steel as constantly as possible to get acceptable qual- ity. But in real application, it is not practical to apply EMS to the whole strand region because of energy consumption and other technical reasons. Many engineers have tried to resolve this prob- lem by using multi-level stirring [9]. But it has multi white band problem and is not energy efﬁcient especially when all the stirrers are operated at the same frequencies. All the above mentioned problems in an EMS process are related to insufﬁcient stirring of molten steel, which is mainly due to the inefﬁciency of magnetic stirring system. Because magnetic ﬂux density decreases signiﬁcantly when passing through metal med- ium [10], we need extra power to stir molten steel. This is one of the reasons why magnetic stirring shows low energy efﬁciency. In the present study, we try to ﬁnd more efﬁcient way to in- crease stirring efﬁciency with limited energy source in an EMS pro- cess. Theoretical backgrounds There has been a great interest in studying magnetohydrody- namic ﬂow due to the effect of magnetic ﬁelds on the performance of many systems using electrically conducting ﬂuids such as elec- trical power generator, cooling of nuclear reactors by liquid so- dium, heat exchangers and MHD accelerators [13,14]. Because we should

--- CHUNK 5 ---
deal with a system of coupled nonlinear partial differen- tial equations to study the interactions between magnetic ﬁeld and ﬂuid ﬂow, most MHD research work have been numerical studies [14–17]. However there are also some analytical approaches for simpliﬁed but practically important systems around porous media [18,19]. Because the kinematic viscosity of liquid metal is close to that of water, almost all ﬂows of interest in electromagnetic stirring are turbulent. The induced current by imposed magnetic ﬁeld makes another magnetic ﬁeld. The movement of conducting ﬂuid gener- ates current in addition to the induced current by applied magnetic ﬁeld. Due to these complexities of coupled magnetohydrodynamic problems, a lot of studies on the ﬂuid ﬂow induced by rotating magnetic ﬁeld have been performed from mid 1960s [20,21]. Re- cent numerical studies directly solve the coupled magnetic and ﬂuid ﬂow equations of an industrial EMS process to handle this complexity [16,22–24]. However even in numerical modeling, we need some simplifying assumptions because of difﬁculties in solv- ing the original hyperbolic Maxwell equations mathematically and numerically. The objective of current study is not the precise simulation of an EMS process but the feasibility test for the stirring enhancement idea based on EMS

--- CHUNK 6 ---
ﬂow characteristics. Therefore, we use the approximate analytical solution [21] for the electromagnetic force rather than solve complex electromagnetic ﬁeld equations. Be- cause the key point in an EMS system is that a rotating magnetic ﬁeld induces a Lorentz force that is independent of the velocity of the liquid metal, and whose dominant force direction is rota- tional [21]. In the magnetic stirring of steel, the Lorentz force is ap- plied over a relatively short portion of the whole long liquid metal column. This differential rotation between the forced and unforced regions of the column induces secondary ﬂow, which plays impor- tant role in electromagnetic stirring of steel [21,22,25]. Using ana- lytic solution for a Lorentz force and numeral model for molten steel ﬂow, we can also take into account this important ﬂow characteristic. Problem description and numerical methods The shear ﬂow near the solid wall induced by a rotating mag- netic ﬁeld in an EMS system plays two major roles in the view point of stirring. One is the fragmentation of the dendrites grown on the inner solid surface and the other is the removal of bubble or solid inclusions trapped between dendrites [7,8]. The former is affected mainly by the macroscopic ﬂow characteristics such as turbulent kinetic energy near the solid wall but the latter is inﬂu- enced by the detailed microscopic ﬂow distribution within bound- ary layer of turbulent ﬂow. Therefore, to investigate the two effects collectively, we have examined the two different systems: the overall macroscopic ﬂow of

The optimal excitation frequency For the enhancement of macroscopic stirring, the optimal fre- quency has been found around the resonant frequency of the sec- ondary rotational ﬂow whereas for the efﬁcient inclusion removal, the optimal frequency has been found near the resonant frequency of the primary rotational ﬂow. For the macroscopic optimal fre- quency, we have a lower limit because a slow change can be inef- ﬁcient. We also have an upper limit for the microscopic

--- CHUNK 26 ---
optimal frequency, because the magnitude of excitation for the microscopic system is limited by the macroscopic ﬂuid ﬂow response. Therefore the overall optimal time-periodic excitation frequency can be found between the resonant frequencies of the two characteristic ﬂows. For the ﬁnal determination of the overall optimal frequency, we have some choices depending on the objective of the time-periodic excitation. If we are more interested in the increase in the macro- scopic mixing rather than inclusion removal, we can choose 0.125 Hz for the overall excitation frequency. Because the micro- scopic optimal frequency has broad range, even under this fre- quency, we can also expect some increase in inclusion removal comparing with non-excitation case. On the other hand, if we are interested in more efﬁcient inclusion removal, we can choose 0.25 Hz for the overall excitation frequency. We have found some possibilities for the stirring enhancement of molten steel based on the time-periodic excitation. However some limitations and improvements of the current research work should be taken into account. Because the macroscopic response to the time-periodic excitation mainly depends on the secondary rotational ﬂow characteristics, the accurate estimation of the sec- ondary ﬂow is important. In this regard, using more realistic

--- CHUNK 27 ---
sys- tem geometry [24], direct calculation of magnetic ﬁeld [22,23], considering temperature distribution [24], and using solidiﬁcation model can improve the modeling accuracy. For the microscopic model, the optimal frequency for better inclusion removal is not only related to the size of the dendrite but also to the geometry and the distance between dendrites. Considering various geome- try, three dimensional ﬂows around dendrite, and the growth of dendrites can be a possible improvement for better modeling. Because the current work is a numerical one, the ﬁeld test using a real EMS process is necessary to check whether the time-periodic excitation makes good effect on the steel quality. However because the strong excitation of molten steel ﬂow can make billet system unstable and provoke rupture of billet skin, a careful conducting of the test is needed. In spite of these limitations and further improvements, the current research work can be one starting point for better control of an EMS system. Because the current approach is not limited to speciﬁc system, similar idea can be used to en- hance stirring efﬁciency in many industrial mixing systems. 5. Conclusion It has been found that time-periodically perturbed magnetic ﬁeld can enhance the stirring efﬁciency

of molten steel and in- crease the uniformity of inclusions. The macroscopic response of molten steel ﬂow to the time-periodic excitation is closely related to the secondary rotational ﬂow and the optimal frequency for the macroscopic stirring of molten steel has been found near the reso- nant frequency of the secondary rotational ﬂow. For the efﬁcient removal of inclusions, the optimal frequency has been found around the resonant frequency of the primary rotational ﬂow. Therefore the overall optimal frequency for both efﬁcient stirring and uniform inclusion distribution can be chosen between the res- onant frequencies of the two characteristic rotational ﬂows.
